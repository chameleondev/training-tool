Aplastic anaemia: general overview
  • Aplastic anaemia is a life-threatening bone marrow failure disorder1
    • Associated with high mortality rates if left untreated
  • Although aplastic anaemia may occur in all age groups, there is a biphasic age distribution, with peaks in two age groups:2
    • Between 10 and 25 years of age
    • In individuals older than 60 years
  • There is no significant difference in incidence between genders2
  • The condition is characterised by:3
    • Pancytopenia (a reduced numbers of all blood cell types)
    • Hypocellular bone marrow in the absence of abnormal infiltration or increased reticulin
  • Aplastic anaemia can be either inherited, or acquired3
Aplastic anaemia: general overview
  • Aplastic anaemia is associated with high mortality rates3
    • Survival rate curves are biphasic:
      • Rapid early mortality, from 73% at 3 months (after diagnosis) to 57% between 2 and 5 years, and 51% at 15 years
    • Survival rates are higher in male patients, although the difference is not significant
    • Survival rates are significantly lower in patients with severe disease
    • Survival rates also decrease with age
    • Disease evolution to myelodysplastic syndrome and leukaemia may occur in ~10–15% of patients with severe aplastic anaemia4
  • Fatality rates are also decreasing with improvement in available therapies (i.e. higher rates in the 1980s compared with the 1990s)3
    • Up until the 1970s and the advent of bone-marrow transplantation and potent immunosuppressive therapy, aplastic anaemia was fatal in most cases within a year of diagnosis5
