Role of thrombopoietin in haematopoiesis
  • Evidence indicates that TPO, acting through TPO-R, is essential for normal thrombopoiesis1
  • TPO-Rs are present on primitive cells, which means TPO is also involved in early haematopoiesis2
    • TPO may act at multiple levels to stimulate haematopoiesis2
      • Can stimulate HSCs to give rise to myeloid progenitors with the potential to differentiate into platelets, RBCs and white blood cells (WBCs)3

Role of thrombopoietin in haematopoiesis
  • The most primitive haematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) are thought to be quiescent, and triggering their growth may require combined stimulation by multiple cytokines2
    • More committed progenitors can be induced to proliferate by specific cytokines
    • Because most mature blood stem cells have a limited life span, the ability of HSCs to perpetuate themselves through self-renewal and generate new blood cells for the lifetime of an organism is critical to sustaining life4
  • Thrombopoietin receptor is present on primitive HSCs and progenitor cells (HPCs)6
    • Thrombopoietin/TPO-R signalling regulates quiescent HSCs in humans
    • People with TPO-R mutations develop congenital amegakaryocytic thrombocytopenia
      • Megakaryocytes in the bone marrow are absent or greatly diminished
  • The role of TPO in haematopoiesis has also been investigated in mouseÂ models5,7
    • Haematopoietic repopulating assays support a role for TPO in HSC survival, self-renewal and expansion8
    • Thrombopoietin receptor knockout mice have reduced numbers of HSCs/HPCs
