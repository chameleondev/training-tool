Aplastic anaemia is a consequence of bone marrow failure
  • Aplastic anaemia is a rare but heterogeneous disorder,1 first described by Paul Ehrlich in 18882
    • It is characterised by bone marrow failure1
      • Aplastic anaemia may also be referred to as a bone marrow failure syndrome3
    • It affects both genders1
  • Although aplastic anaemia may occur in all age groups, there is a biphasic age distribution, with peaks in two age groups:1
    • Between 10 and 25 years of age
    • In individuals older than 60 years
  • Aplastic anaemia is associated with premature mortality3
  • In ~10–15% of cases, the precipitating factor is known3
    • For example, chemotherapy or infections (including hepatitis viruses)
  • In ~70% of cases, aplastic anaemia is idiopathic3
Aplastic anaemia is a consequence of bone marrow failure
  • Aplastic anaemia/bone marrow failure syndromes refers to the inability of the bone marrow to produce healthy levels of blood cells3
  • The condition is characterised by:4
    • Pancytopenia (a reduced number of all blood cell types, e.g. red blood cells, neutrophils and platelets)4
    • Hypocellular bone marrow in the absence of abnormal infiltration or increased reticulin4
  • Aplastic anaemia can be either inherited or acquired4
