Definitive care in severe aplastic anaemia:
immunosuppressive therapy
  • IST is the first line of definitive treatment for severe aplastic anaemia patients who lack a matched sibling donor or may not be good candidates for HSCT1
  • Haematopoietic response to IST ranges from 50% to 80%1,2
  • The standard regimen consists of horse antithymocyte globulin (h-ATG) and ciclosporin (CsA)1,3
    • The precise mechanism of action of ATG remains unclear4
    • Rabbit ATG (r-ATG) became more frequently used instead of h-ATG for the following reasons5
      • Greater lymphotoxicity of r-ATG versus h-ATG
      • Efficacy of r-ATG as a salvage therapy for relapsed or refractory severe aplastic anaemia6,7
    • However, a pivotal, large prospective study suggested poorer outcomes following r-ATG compared with h-ATG, which prompted a revival of h-ATG use8
  • Relapse after IST may occur in a third of patients with severe aplastic anaemia9
  • Development of cytogenetic abnormalities and associated clinical disorders usually occurs within 2–4 years of IST10
Definitive care in severe aplastic anaemia: immunosuppressive therapy
  • ATG is a polyclonal immunoglobulin known to react with a number of cell types including T cells and natural killer cells, as well as molecules that modulate the immune response11
    • It is thought to block lymphocyte mediated suppression of haematopoiesis
  • Ciclosporin inhibits T-cell activation by blocking the transcription of cytokine genes involved in inflammation, including IL-212
  • ATG administration may cause adverse effects including:10
    • Serum sickness 1–2 weeks after administration of ATG
    • Increases in liver enzymes; these normalise over several days
  • CsA may cause:10
    • Hypertension
    • Gingival hyperplasia, particularly when calcium channel blockers are co-administered to treat CsA-associated hypertension
    • Corticosteroids or antihistamines may be co-administered with an ATG infusion to reduce the risk of an allergic reaction3
  • Cytogenetic abnormalities in severe aplastic anaemia occurs at an approximate rate of 10–15% per 10 years13
    • It may be due to inadequate IST, which results in refractory severe aplastic anaemia and preservation of genetically abnormal cells6,14
    • Disorders resulting from these cytogenetic abnormalities include monosomy 7, high-grade MDS, complex karyotype and leukaemia10
