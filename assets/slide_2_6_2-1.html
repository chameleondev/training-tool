Definitive care in severe aplastic anaemia:
haematopoietic stem cell transplant
  • HSCT is potentially curative and restores haematopoiesis in patients with severe aplastic anaemia1,2
  • It is the treatment of choice for patients with severe aplastic anaemia who have an HLA-matched sibling donor1,2
    • HSCT is also associated with a reduced risk of relapse and evolution of late clonal disorders (e.g. MDS and PNH)2,3
  • The main source of stem cells include mobilised peripheral blood and bone marrow1
    • Cord blood has been successfully explored as a source of stem cells, but is not the first choice4
  • Survival 5 years after HSCT may be:1
    • Over 70% for patients aged ≤40 years
    • ~50–60% for patients aged >40 years

Definitive care in severe aplastic anaemia: haematopoietic stem cell transplant
  • HSCT is the only potential cure for aplastic anaemia to date6
  • Fewer than 30% of patients will have an HLA-matched sibling donor7
  • Survival after transplantation has increased steadily since the 1970s, but complications such as chronic graft-versus-host disease (GVHD; also known as transplant rejection) occur in up to one-third of patients7
    • Risk of GVHD increases with age, which accounts for decreased survival and long-term morbidity in older patients1,7
  • HSCT from a matched sibling donor is the current standard of care in severe aplastic anaemia1
    • Although alternative donor transplantation may be pursued, survival is variable and not as high as that observed with donation from a matched sibling (43–94% vs survival consistently over 70% in patients up to the age of 40)

Haematopoietic stem cell transplant
  • HSCT is the treatment of choice for children and young adults (aged up to 40 years) with severe aplastic anaemia who have an HLA-matched sibling donor1
  • Most candidates are not suitable for optimal HSCT for reasons such as:
    • Lack of a matched sibling donor1
      • Fewer than 30% of patients will have an HLA-matched sibling donor2
    • Lead time to identify a suitable unrelated donor (UD) 1
    • Age1
    • Comorbidities1
      • Contraindications include poor cardiac and or renal function, and the presence of HIV or active hepatitis B or C viral infection3
    • Lack of access to transplantation facilities1

Unrelated donor transplantation studies
  • Survival after HLA-matched sibling transplantation can be >80% (for patients aged 20 years or under)9
  • Outcomes with unrelated donor (UD) HSCT have improved because of more stringent donor selection1
    • Facilitated by precision molecular typing, more effective conditioning regimens and a higher quality transfusion and antimicrobial supportive care1
  • However, experience from large cohort studies suggests that the outcome with unrelated HSCT is still not as favourable as that of a matched donor1
  • 3–5-year survival is highly variable and ranges between 43% and 94%, whereas a study of patients with severe aplastic anaemia (n=1300) who received transplants from a matched sibling donor showed that survival at 5 years was >70% in patients between the ages of 20 and 401

Umbilical cord blood transplantation
  • Umbilical cord blood (UCB) is attractive as an alternative to unrelated-adult donor grafts, because placental lymphocytes are immunologically naïve1
    • This would allow for transplantation of UCB units with degrees of donor-recipient disparity that would be undesirable when using bone marrow or peripheral blood grafts
  • However, the outcomes of UCB transplantation in patients with severe aplastic anaemia are not encouraging1
    • Unpublished data from the Center for International Blood and Marrow Transplant Research (CIBMTR) show an observed graft failure rate of 30% was higher and a 2-year overall survival rate of 35%1
      • Graft failure rate is higher, and 2-year overall survival rate lower than those seen with HLA-matched or mismatched bone marrow grafts1
    • Data from Eurocord (a European organisation promoting research into UCB biology and medical applications of cord cells) also suggest high graft failure rates and 2-year overall survival of approximately 40% associated with unrelated UCB transplantation2

Transplantation protocols
Transplantation protocols
  • There have been different drug combinations over the years, but the popular regimen (also the current standard) includes:
    • Cy + ATG (pre-transplant)1,2
      • However, a randomised controlled trial found that this combination was not superior to Cy alone4
    • CsA and methotrexate for GVHD prophylaxis3
  • Adding fludarabine (Flu) to Cy/ATG has been shown to overcome the risk of rejection in heavily transfused or older patients undergoing matched sibling HSCT, and may be an option in this patient group2,5
  • Alemtuzumab has been investigated as a replacement for ATG in transplant protocols and may mitigate the impact of GVHD6
    • 2-year survival of >80% for both matched sibling and unrelated HSCT6
    • Acute GVHD was observed in 13.5% of patients; 4% developed GVHD6
  • When considering UD HSCT, the following should be noted:2
    • It is currently unclear what the optimal conditioning regimen is in the UD setting
    • Low-dose total body irradiation may be included in pre-transplant conditioning to ensure engraftment (transplant acceptance) and to potentially reduce long-term risks associated with radiation exposure at a young age
