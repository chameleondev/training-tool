Stem cell depletion may limit the response to
immunosuppressive therapy
  • Blood counts may reflect stem cell reserve and have been proposed as predictors of response to non-transplantation therapy1
  • A lack of response to IST may be due to the limited number of haematopoietic stem cells that are also unable to maintain the haematopoietic function of the bone marrow1
  • IST in combination with a factor to stimulate stem cell production may overcome the limit of response to IST alone1
  • However, growth factors, including G-CSF and erythropoietin, have had limited utility in aplastic anaemia2
    • Additional strategies to stimulate stem cell proliferation are being explored

Definitive care in severe aplastic anaemia: immunosuppressive therapy modifications
  • Although post-HSCT survival has improved over the years, response rate to IST has not changed as substantially with time8
  • Modifications to conventional IST include the addition of danazol,2 mycophenolate mofetil,3 sirolimus4 or haematopoietic growth factors5
    • None of these therapies has significantly improved or decreased relapse rates
    • G-CSF may be used during infectious episodes in neutropenic patients9
  • A 10-year follow-up study of high-dose cyclophosphamide suggested that it is an effective and potentially curative, non-transplant treatment option:6
    • Treatment-naïve severe aplastic anaemia: 10-year survival: 88%; response rate 72%; event-free survival: 58%6
    • Refractory severe aplastic anaemia: 10-year survival: 62%; response rate: 48%; event-free survival: 27%6
  • Corticosteroids are ineffective in aplastic anaemia9
    • They encourage bacterial and fungal colonisation, and can precipitate serious gastrointestinal haemorrhage in the presence of severe thrombocytopenia
