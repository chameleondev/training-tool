Bone marrow stroma
  • The bone marrow stroma provides the microenvironment and growth factors needed to facilitate haematopoeisis,1,2 including:
    • Interleukins (ILs)3
    • Colony-stimulating factors (CSFs)3
    • Erythropoietin (EPO)3
    • Thrombopoietin (TPO)3
  • Thrombopoietin was originally thought to be specific to platelet maturation from MK cells, but has been shown to promote survival of all haematopoietic stem cells (HSCs)4,5
    • All HSCs express TPO receptors (TPO-Rs; also known as MpL)6
Thrombopoietin and thrombopoietin receptors
  • Binding of TPO to TPO-Rs on the surface of MK cells triggers the proliferation and maturation cascade that results in mature circulating platelets1
  • However, the expression of TPO-R on all HSCs suggests that TPO has a broader role in HSC survival and haematopoiesis, in addition to platelet generation1,2
  • Cell culture studies have shown that TPO activity may not be lineage-specific2
  • TPO-R deficiencies/defects are associated with haematological diseases
    • In mice: bone marrow aplasia3
    • In humans:
      • Multi-lineage marrow failure associated with congenital amegakaryocytic thrombocytopenia4
      • Familial aplastic anaemia5
The thrombopoietin receptor is expressed at all stages of stem cell differentiation
TPO-R is expressed in cells from the uncommitted progenitor stem cell stage to specific cell lineages, including megakaryocytes
