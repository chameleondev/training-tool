Supportive care in severe aplastic anaemia: burden of treatment
  • Although transplant options must be pursued in the event of persistent pancytopenia despite IST, supportive care strategies may still have to be used1
  • Transfusions may be implemented to correct anaemia and thrombocytopenia1
    • However, repeated red blood cell transfusions can lead to iron overload, causing significant damage to internal organs and increasing mortality risk2
  • Broad-spectrum parenteral antibiotics may be required when fever or documented infection occurs in the presence of severe neutropenia1
    • G-CSF may be used to stimulate a neutrophil response in the event of severe infection3
  • Growth factors, including G-CSF and EPO, may be used to support blood counts, although evidence does not suggest that they improve relapse rates or survival3â€“5
Supportive care in severe aplastic anaemia: burden of treatment
  • Although transfusions may be used to correct anaemia and thrombocytopenia, post-transfusion complications may occur, including:
    • Acute haemolytic transfusion reaction in the event of blood type (ABO) incompatibility6,7
      • Symptoms include fever, hypotension and/or dyspnoea within minutes to hours of a transfusion6
      • Reactions may also occur up to 28 days after a transfusion6
      • Anaphylaxis6,8
        • An important cause of transfusion-related morbidity6
        • Most allergic and anaphylactic reactions are due to platelet-derived microparticles8
      • Development of antibodies against blood cell antigens (including human leukocyte [HLA] and platelet [HPA] antigens), also known as alloimmunisation, may occur following regular blood product transfusions (particularly platelets)6,7
        • Anti-HPA antibody development may result in refractivity to donor platelet transfusions,6 rendering an important supportive strategy ineffective
