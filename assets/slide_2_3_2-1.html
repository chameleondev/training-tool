Pathophysiology of acquired aplastic anaemia: from a normal to aberrant immune response
Somatic mutations in T cells may drive the autoimmune destruction of the bone marrow

It is thought that somatic mutations in T cells may drive an abnormal immune response. Deregulation of T-cell mediated immunity and inflammatory signalling cascades results in tissue damage and destruction

Pathophysiology of acquired aplastic anaemia: from a normal to aberrant immune response
  • In a normal immune response, the inflammatory response and tissue damage resolve once the stimulating antigen is withdrawn
  • In immune-mediated disease, acquired mutations that may alter T-lymphocyte proliferation, susceptibility of genetically defective T cells to apoptosis or regulation of cytokine signalling pathways can lead to:
    • Persistence of mutant T-cell clones
    • Further recruitment of T cells and other immune cells
    • Exposure of new antigens
    • Tissue damage and destruction

Aberrant immune response directed at haematopoietic stem cells of the bone marrow
  • Evidence suggests that blood and bone marrow from patients with aplastic anaemia contains an increased number of activated T cells1
  • Activated T cells produce lymphokines, including:
    • Interferon-γ (IFN-γ)1,2
    • Tumour necrosis factor (TNF)1,2
    • Interleukin 2 (IL-2)1
  • IFN-γ and TNF upregulate each other’s cellular receptors and also induce expression of the Fas receptor1,2 on CD34+ cells2
  • Increased IL-2 production leads to polyclonal expansion of T cells1

Thrombopoietin and thrombopoietin receptors
  • Cell culture studies have shown that TPO activity may not be lineage-specific2
    • In suspended cell culture: TPO promotes HSC survival but not proliferation2
    • Culture medium + IL-3/stem cell factor (SCF): accelerates HSC entry into the cell cycle, resulting in a high output of colony-forming cells of all lineages2,6
    • Receptor expression studies: in addition to MK cells, TPO-Rs (also known as MpL) may be expressed on a variety of cells derived from the bone marrow7,8 (e.g. CD34+ cells2,8)

Destruction of the bone marrow may occur through two mechanisms
  • Activation of the Fas receptor by the Fas ligand present on cytotoxic T-lymphocytes leads to apoptosis (programmed cell death) of target cells1
  • IFN-γ may affect immune destruction of HSCs in two ways1
    • Via interferon regulator factor 1 (IRF-1), which inhibits transcription of cellular genes that control entry into the cell cycle
    • Induction of cellular genes (e.g. inducible nitric oxide synthase; iNOS), which contributes to a cytotoxic environment
HSCs may be destroyed by direct induction of
apoptosis or inhibition of cell cycle entry1
