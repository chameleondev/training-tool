Natural history of acquired aplastic anaemia
  • Patients with severe aplastic anaemia are usually asymptomatic prior to diagnosis1
    • They commonly seek medical attention because of symptoms of anaemia or haemorrhage
    • Even if the patient has severe neutropenia, infection is uncommon at first presentation
    • Initial reduction in blood count may be associated with a single blood cell lineage (single cell cytopenia)
      • Commonly thrombocytopenia or anaemia
  • A known complication of severe aplastic anaemia is the appearance of cytogenetic abnormalities in the haematopoietic stem cells of the bone marrow (a process also referred to as clonal evolution)2
    • For this reason, cytogenetic testing is performed in all studies of severe aplastic anaemia conducted by the National Institutes of Health (NIH)
  • The clinical consequences of cytogenetic abnormalities are variable, depending on the presence or absence of clinical sequelae such as dysplasia or worsening cytopenias2
    • Severe aplastic anaemia may progress to myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) or leukaemia at a rate of ~10–20% per 5–10 years,1–3 and evolution to MDS can occur either in early- or late-stage aplastic anaemia2
  • Prognosis is poorer in severe aplastic anaemia patients who develop cytogenetic abnormalities compared with those who do not1
Natural history of acquired aplastic anaemia2
  • As cytogenetically normal haematopoietic stem cells become depleted, cells containing chromosomal abnormalities may be retained rather than destroyed by the immune system
  • Ongoing depletion of haematopoietic stem cells creates a selection pressure that allows the this abnormal cell population to be preserved and to expand at a rapid rate
  • The presence of mutant clones can eventually lead to disorders that arise from chromosomal abnormalities, including MDS, leukaemia and paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria

Cytogenetic abnormalities in severe acquired aplastic anaemia
  • Acquired aplastic anaemia may co-exist with, or evolve into, clonal disorders1
  • HSC depletion may cause the recruitment of a genetically defective clone2
    • Paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria (PNH)1
      • ~40–50% of patients with acquired aplastic anaemia have higher PNH cell counts
      • Classic PNH can evolve in to marrow failure
      • All patients with PNH show evidence of haematopoietic deficiency
    • MDS1
      • Aneuploidy (abnormal chromosome number)
      • Usually monosomy 7 and trisomy 8
        • Monosomy 7 is associated with refractory cytopenia or AML
Potential consequences of bone marrow injury3
Cytogenetic abnormalities in severe acquired aplastic anaemia
  • Bone marrow injury may be associated with three possible consequences:3
    • Generation of an autoimmune reaction against HSCs or progenitors
    • Induction of a genetic mutation in HSCs resulting in clonal expansion
    • Both an autoimmune reaction against, and clonal expansion of, HSCs
  • Depending on the rate of clonal growth, clonality may occur:3
    • Simultaneously with the autoimmune attack (aplastic anaemia/paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria overlap or hypoplastic myelodysplasia)
    • Subsequent to the autoimmune attack (secondary clonal disease)
  • Cytogenically abnormal clones may:2
    • Trigger an autoimmune tumour surveillance response that eliminates the abnormal cells, but also destroys healthy HSCs
    • Create a selection pressure that leads to the generation and escape of mutant clones that outgrow normal haematopoiesis
