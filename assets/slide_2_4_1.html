Diagnosis of acquired aplastic anaemia: overview
  • All new patients presenting with acquired aplastic anaemia should be assessed to:1
    • Confirm the diagnosis and exclude other causes of pancytopenia and hypocellular bone marrow
    • Classify disease severity using the blood and bone marrow criteria
    • Document the presence of associated paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria (PNH) and cytogenetic clones
    • Exclude late-onset inherited bone marrow failure syndromes
  • Despite the precision of the diagnostic criteria for acquired aplastic anaemia, it is still a diagnosis of exclusion2
  • In some patients, blood count may remain stable for years and patients may be asymptomatic3
    • Moderate aplastic anaemia may not require treatment4
  • Patients commonly seek medical attention and a diagnosis when they experience symptoms of anaemia or bleeding, by which time pancytopenia and HSC deficiency has worsened4
Diagnosis of acquired aplastic anaemia: overview
  • Distinction between acquired aplastic anaemia and other disorders such as MDS can be challenging, particularly in older patients3
  • Patients commonly seek medical attention and a diagnosis when they experience symptoms of anaemia or bleeding, by which time pancytopenia and HSC deficiency has worsened4
    • Severe aplastic anaemia almost always requires treatment4
  • Patient outcome is related to the time from diagnosis to treatment2
