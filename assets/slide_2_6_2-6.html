Treatment of refractory severe aplastic anaemia
  • Refractory aplastic anaemia is defined as lack of response with persistence of severe pancytopenia at 6 months after one course of IST1
    • Approximately one-third of patients with severe aplastic anaemia fail to respond to IST2
  • HSCT should be considered if pancytopenia persists 6 months after IST3
    • First choice in older and younger patients: matched sibling donor HSCT3
    • Unrelated donor (UD) HSCT may be considered in younger patients3
  • Survival rates have improved over time4

Aplastic anaemia is a consequence of bone marrow failure
  • Treatment approach will depend on factors including the patient’s age and the availability of a histocompatible donor3
  • A second course of IST may be preferable to a high-risk transplant from a mismatched,5 unrelated,5 haploidentical7 or umbilical cord donor7
    • Response to r-ATG may be 27–77%8,9
  • Alemtuzumab has also been investigated for use in refractory severe aplastic anaemia, with a response rate of 37% (compared with 33% for r-ATG)10
  • Before the advent of bone-marrow transplantation and IST in the 1970s, aplastic anaemia was fatal in most cases within a year of diagnosis11
    • Survival following haematopoietic stem cell transplantation may be as high as 80% if a matched sibling donor can be found12
  • Data suggests that survival following IST has not improved to the same degree as haematopoietic stem cell transplantation13
    • This may be cause IST regimens have not changed over time13
    • Observable improvements may be attributed to better antimicrobial support, which has reduced infection-related mortality14
