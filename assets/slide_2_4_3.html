Differential diagnosis of pancytopenia
  • No single test can provide the physician with a reliable diagnosis of acquired (idiopathic) aplastic anaemia1
  • However, advances in disease awareness and testing technologies have facilitated the diagnosis of other disorders that may cause pancytopenia, such as:1
    • Genetic testing
    • Routine cytogenic testing
    • Serological studies
    • Innovations in diagnostic algorithms
  • Highly sensitive assays are valuable in the differential diagnosis of:2
    • PNH2
    • MDS2
    • Aplastic crisis associated with sickle cell anaemia3

Differential diagnosis: aplastic crisis in sickle cell anaemia vs acquired aplastic anaemia
  • When diagnosing acquired aplastic anaemia, one possible diagnosis of exclusion may be sickle cell-related aplastic crisis
  • Sickle cell disease (SCD) and its variants are genetic disorders resulting from the presence of a mutated form of haemoglobin, haemoglobin S1
    • This causes red blood cells to distort into a characteristic sickle shape
  • The type of anaemia associated with this illness is caused by haemolysis of the red blood cells because of their sickle shape1
  • Haemolytic anaemia places patients with SCD at risk of developing severe anaemia when erythropoiesis is disrupted by parvovirus B19 infection, causing:1
    • Aplastic crisis
    • Sudden reduction in packed cell volume
    • Reticulocytopenia
    • Bone marrow devoid of erythroid precursors
  • An aplastic crisis in SCD is transient and recovery occurs after blood transfusion1
  • Acquired aplastic anaemia deteriorates over time2
    • Haematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT) is the only potential cure for severe aplastic anaemia
