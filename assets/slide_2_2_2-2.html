Classification of aplastic anaemia
Classification Criteria
Non-severe (moderate) Hypocellular bone marrow with peripheral blood values do not meet the criteria for severe or very severe aplastic anaemia
Severe

Bone marrow cellularity equal to 25% of the normal control, or 25–50% with <30% residual HSCs

AND two out of three of the following peripheral blood criteria:

  • Neutrophil count: <0.5×109/l
  • Platelet count: <20×109/l
  • Reticulocyte count: <20×109/l
Very severe Severe aplastic anaemia with neutrophil count <0.2×109/l

Aplastic anaemia: characteristics in children versus adults
  • Outcome of transplant influences outcome, with cure rates of 70–90% in patients younger than 30 years, and transplant-related mortality in older patients (>40 years) being ~50%5
    • This is due to the risk of graft-versus-host disease (GVHD), which increases steadily with age
    • Transplant-related mortality and the risk of GVHD are approximately two-times higher with unrelated donors than with matched sibling donors
  • Adult manifestation of genetic aplastic anaemia is uncommon, as the majority of patients with these types of anaemia (e.g. Fanconi anaemia) present towards the end of the first decade of life, and only those with atypical presentations such as myelodysplasia are being first diagnosed in adulthood3
