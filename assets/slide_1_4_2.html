Principles of autoimmunity
  • Autoimmune disease results from failure of the immune system to recognise “self”1
    • Estimated to affect 3–5% of the worldwide population1
  • Environmental exposure to drugs, viruses and toxins is thought to trigger autoimmunity; however, most cases are idiopathic2
  • Primary drivers of autoimmune diseases are considered to be CD4+ T-lymphocytes, which modify the responses of other immune cells1
  • Pathophysiology of aplastic anaemia is thought to be immune-mediated in most cases2
T-cell-signalling and autoimmunity:
possible molecular mechanisms of disease
  • There are three main cell-oriented pathways for preventing autoimmunity:
    • Deletion: removal of self-reactive T cells
    • Anergy: APC presents self-antigen but no co-stimulatory signal
    • Suppression: clonal suppression by factors derived from other leukocytes
  • In autoimmune disease, a breakdown in any of these mechanisms can commonly occur
    • Similar principles apply to B-cell self-tolerance
  • The combination of innate and acquired immune mechanisms results in an imbalance between pro-inflammatory and anti-inflammatory signals and direction of immune cell attack against “self”
