Erythropoiesis
  • Feedback mechanism mediated by erythropoietin
  • Reduced numbers of circulating RBCs leads to lowered Hb
    • Lowered Hb leads to tissue hypoxia
  • Tissue hypoxia induces EPO synthesis in the kidneys
  • EPO binds to receptors on RBC progenitor cells (burst-forming units-erythroid and colony-forming units [CFUs]-erythroid)
    • Initiates a cascade of events leading to increased release of RBCs from the bone marrow
  • Increased numbers of Hb-carrying RBCs improves O2 delivery to tissues, after which EPO synthesis is reduced

Embryonic and fetal haematopoiesis
  • Human haematopoiesis begins outside the embryo
    (in the yolk sac) at ~3 weeks1
    • Erythrocyte and granulocyte progenitors can be detected in the yolk sac at Day 25 of development
    • Yolk sac haematopoiesis ceases after 60 days
  • Onset of circulation allows yolk sac-derived blood cells to enter fetal tissues1
    • Liver: first tissue to be colonised
      • Main blood-forming tissue at embryo stage before initiation of marrow haematopoiesis
        (at ~4.5–5 weeks)
  • Bone marrow haematopoiesis starts during Week 11 of development1
    • Last tissue to develop during ontogenesis

Embryonic and fetal haematopoiesis2
  • Haematopoiesis occurs at different anatomical sites during development
  • Embryonic stage: RBC precursors can be recognised as early as 2 weeks; the main type of haemoglobin present is Hb Gower I
  • Fetal liver: main source of fetal blood cells until ~30 weeks. Main type of Hb is fetal haemoglobin (HbF)
  • Spleen: blood cell production from ~10 weeks’ gestation, but used as a secondary source of fetal blood cells
  • After the formation of bone cavities, the bone marrow becomes the only source of blood cells in humans (complete by 40 weeks)
  • Transition to bone marrow haematopoiesis is associated with gradual replacement of HbF with adult Hb (HbA), which contains two α subunits and two β subunits
