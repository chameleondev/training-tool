Adaptive immunity: cell-mediated response
  • There are two key types of T-lymphocytes that participate in cell-mediated immunity1:
    • T-helper (Th) cells (CD4)
    • Cytotoxic (Tc) cells (CD8)
  • T cells are activated when surface receptors bind to recognised antigens1
    • Antigen is bound to the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) on antigen-presenting cells (APCs)
  • Activated Th cells:1
    • Facilitate macrophage and Tc responses
    • Present antigens to B-cells as part of the humoral response
  • Activated Tc cells:
    • Release granules containing cytotoxic proteins, which induces apoptosis of the target cell2

T-cell-mediated immune response
  • Hallmarks of adaptive immunity include antigen specificity and memory1
  • Distinction between ‘‘self’’ and ‘‘non-self’’ by the immune system depends on intricate, well-regulated interactions between APCs and effector cells2
    • Ensures that “self” structures are not attacked2
  • T cells cannot recognise whole antigens in the same way as B-cells, but instead, T cells recognise short peptide fragments on APCs: the MHC1
  • CD4 and CD8 T cells express unique T-cell receptors (TCRs) that determine which MHC–antigen complex is recognised1
