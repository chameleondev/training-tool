Glossary

Adaptive immunity: targeted immune response against pathogens that have been previously encountered by the immune system. Deregulation of this response leads to autoimmunity

Antigen-presenting cell: an assorted group of immune cells that mediate the cellular immune response by processing and presenting antigens to the T cells

B cell: a type of lymphocyte that produce antibodies against antigens; B cells act as antigen-presenting cells and develop into memory cells after activation by antigen interaction

Bone marrow: a flexible tissue in the interior of bones, consisting of yellow marrow (mainly fat cells) and red marrow (mainly haematopoietic tissue). It is the site of haematopoiesis in adults

Colony-forming units: haematopoietic progenitor cells of various types, which differentiate into pre-determined mature cell types, following stimulation by cytokines and other factors

Colony-stimulating factors: glycoproteins that stimulate stem cells in the bone marrow to promote the production of white blood cells (mainly granulocytes) in response to infection

Cytokines: a diverse family of soluble proteins and peptides that regulate cellular functions and interactions. Haematopoietic growth factors are a subset of cytokines

CD4: a glycoprotein expressed on the surface of immune cells such as T-helper cells, monocytes and macrophages

CD8: a glycoprotein predominantly expressed on the surface of cytotoxic T cells

Erythrocyte: red blood cell

Erythropoietin: a glycoprotein hormone produced by interstitial fibroblasts in the kidney to regulate red blood cell production in the bone marrow

Extracellular matrix: a structural network within the bone marrow that serves as an anchor for haematopoietic and non-haematopoietic cells

Granulocyte: a collective term for neutrophils, eosinophils and basophils, which are characterised by the presence of cytoplasmic granules

Haematopoiesis: the formation of blood cellular components from haematopoietic stem cells

Haematopoietic stem cells: totipotent cells that can differentiate to form lymphoid or myeloid pluripotent cells. Pluripotent stem cells give rise to different stem cells committed to a single blood cell line

Innate immunity: also known as non-specific immunity; first line of host defence from infection by other organisms in a non-specific manner

Interleukins: a group of cytokines secreted by immune cells which mediate inter and intra-cellular signalling within the immune system. Interleukins are fundamental to the regulation of both the cellular and inflammatory immune response, cellular development, growth and differentiation

Leukocyte: white blood cell

Lymphocyte: the key cellular component of the adaptive immune system. The three major types are T cells, B cells and natural killer cells

Major histocompatibility complex: cell surface molecules that mediate leukocyte interactions. MHC class II mediates immunisation against a given antigen and MHC I mediates destruction of host cells displaying that antigen

Megakaryocyte: a progenitor cell of the platelet lineage

Phagocytosis: a mechanism by which pathogens and cell debris are removed

Plasma: a pale yellow solution of electrolytes, proteins and small molecules including glucose

Pancytopenia: a reduction in the number of red cells, white cells and platelets

Reticulocyte: an immature red blood cell

Stroma: all tissues not directly involved in the marrow’s primary function of haematopoiesis

T cell: a type of lymphocyte that plays a central role in cell-mediated immunity. Subsets of T cells have distinct functions

Thrombocytes: also known as platelets

Thrombopoietin: a glycoprotein hormone produced by the liver and kidney that regulates the production of platelets by the bone marrow

LEARNING OBJECTIVES
  • By the end of this module you should be able to:
    • Describe the key components of the blood and their main characteristics
      • To understand how blood composition differs in healthy and disease states
    • Understand the origin of these components and how their production is regulated
      • This will help to elucidate the mechanism of action of eltrombopag (detailed in Module 3)
    • Discuss the role of the bone marrow in haematopoiesis
      • This will help to understand how conditions such as aplastic anaemia arise from bone marrow failure (detailed in Module 2)
    • Explain the key processes involved in the human immune response and the proposed mechanisms behind autoimmunity
      • It is thought that acquired aplastic anaemia is associated with autoimmune destruction of the bone marrow
