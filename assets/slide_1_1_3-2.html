White blood cell subtypes and their functions
Each WBC subtype has a distinctive function and role in immunity
  • Neutrophils1
  • ~60% of circulating white cells
  • Multi-lobed nucleus
  • Responsible for non-specific defence against bacterial and fungal infection
  • Undergo apoptosis within 24-48 hours if they do not encounter microbes, but can survive in tissue for several days
  • Lymphocytes1
  • ~33% of circulating white cells
  • Large round nucleus surrounded by a thin rim of cytoplasm
  • Variable lifespan from a few days to years
  • Responsible for antigen immunity, through antibody production
  • Monocytes1
  • <5% of circulating white cells1
  • Kidney-shaped nucleus
  • Immature monocytes circulate in blood for ~10 hours before they enter tissues and mature into macrophages1
  • Tissue macrophages participate in non-specific immunity, and in processing and presentation of antigen to T-lymphocytes as part of specific immunity1
  • Eosinophils1
  • ~1% of circulating white cells1
  • Bi-lobed nucleus1
  • Spend only 18 hours in peripheral circulation2 but can survive under epithelial surfaces (e.g. skin, gut, respiratory tract) for up to 14 days in tissue1
  • Further study is needed into role in parasite preservation and immune response against viral, bacterial and fungal infections 1,2
  • Basophils1
  • <1% of circulating white cells1
  • Mediation of anaphylaxis, hypersensitivity and inflammatory reactions1,3
  • Granules contain histamine and other inflammatory mediators1,3
