Acquired aplastic anaemia treatment: for whom and when?
  • Non-severe aplastic anaemia:
    • Variable clinical course, but seldom life-threatening1
    • Patients may have moderate cytopenia but are asymptomatic2
    • Supportive care with anabolic/low-dose steroids or CsA can be offered2
  • These patients may eventually demonstrate declining peripheral blood counts over time (months)2
  • Severe aplastic anaemia almost always requires treatment3
  • Supportive measures include:1
    • Transfusions of blood products (e.g. red blood cells and platelets) to:
      • Correct or avoid anaemia-associated cardiopulmonary complications
      • Prevent spontaneous bleeding
      • Support recovery in the event of infection
    • Prophylactic antibiotics
      • Fungal and bacterial infections are a major cause of death in patients with severe aplastic anaemia
    • Treatment with growth factors (e.g. G-CSF) to stimulate neutrophil response
  • Definitive treatment: haematopoiesis can be restored in severe aplastic anaemia after HSCT3

Supportive care considerations in
severe aplastic anaemia
  • The type of supportive care required for an individual depends on the severity of their symptoms and pancytopenia1
  • Evidence supporting the use of human growth factors to sustain blood counts in severe aplastic anaemia is mixed1
    • G-CSF may be used to stimulate a neutrophil response in the presence of severe infection and as an adjunct to immunosuppressive therapy (IST)1
      • However, data suggest that G-CSF may not modify overall haematological response or survival1
    • A potential concern is that the use of recombinant human erythropoietin (rHuEPO) may increase the risk of the patient developing anti-rHuEPO antibodies2,3
      • This may result in a sudden worsening of anaemia due to red cell aplasia2,3
    • IST is the first-line therapy for severe aplastic anaemia patients >40 years or <40 years of age who lack a human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-compatible sibling4
  • Repeated transfusion of blood products can lead to iron overload, causing significant damage to internal organs and increasing mortality risk5
    • Current evidence suggests that daily chelation with deferasirox may be effective in reducing serum ferritin and liver transaminase levels, and that it is a well-tolerated therapy5
