New approaches in severe aplastic anaemia treatment
  • The goal in severe aplastic anaemia is to find treatments with fewer side-effects than current options
  • Therapeutic agents currently being investigated for severe aplastic anaemia are:
    • Eltrombopag: Phase II1,2
      • Oral thrombopoietin mimetic 2
    • Alemtuzumab: Phase III2,3
      • Humanised anti-CD52 IgG1 monoclonal antibody (immunosuppressive agent)2

New approaches in severe aplastic anaemia treatment
  • The National Heart Lung and Blood Institute (NHLBI) is conducting trials for alemtuzumab and daclizumab
  • Alemtuzumab is being investigated in Phase III trials as monotherapy for treatment-naïve, relapsed and refractory severe aplastic anaemia3
    • It is a humanised monoclonal antibody directed against CD52 antigen on the surface of mature T cells
    • Refractory patients: response rate: 37%; 3-year survival: 83%
    • Relapsed patients: response rate: 56%; 3-year survival: 86%
  • Daclizumab is being investigated in Phase II trials for the treatment of moderate aplastic anaemia4
    • It acts by blocking the action of IL-2

Unmet needs in the treatment of severe aplastic
anaemia
  • Stem cell transplantation and IST are the current options for the treatment of aplastic anaemia1
    • However, fewer than 30% of patients will have an HLA-matched sibling donor2
    • Transplant is associated with potentially life-threatening complications including GVHD2
  • Patients may relapse or become refractory to IST1
    • A second course with rabbit ATG and CSA may be associated with variable response rates of 27–77% but this is still inferior to the responses seen in the relapsed setting3,4
    • Additional treatment options for refractory disease are limited
  • Long-term supportive care may be associated with a significant transfusion burden5
    • Repeated transfusion of blood products to maintain cell counts may lead to iron overload and damage to internal organs
    • Alloimmunisation may limit transfusion efficacy after repeated use6
  • Disease progression to cytogenetic disorders such as MDS and leukemia7 occurs at a rate of 10–20% per 10 years7,8 in patients with severe aplastic anaemia

Investigational therapies in severe aplastic anaemia:
developments in stem cell transplantation
  • Alternative stem cell sources have been investigated intensively, because many patients lack a histocompatible unrelated donor for HSCT1
    • Unrelated donor (UD) bone marrow HSCT results in higher rates of GVHD1–3
    • UD blood HSCT results in graft rejection, poor immune reconstitution and susceptibility to viral reactivations1,4,5
  • Methods to improve the feasibility of haploidentical donors for HSCT include:
    • Manipulation of the graft before infusion to reduce rates of GVHD6
      • Selective removal of T cells and repleting non-CD34+ cells6
    • Post-transplant administration of high-dose cyclophosphamide (50 mg/kg/day)7
  • Evidence for the combination of haploidentical and umbilical cord blood cells is promising8

Limited advances in novel non-transplant therapies for
severe aplastic anaemia
  • The emphasis of most IST trials in severe aplastic anaemia has been in:
    • Adding different immunosuppressants, including mycophenolate mofetil or sirolimus
    • Using more potent lymphotoxic agents such as r-ATG1
      • Neither modification has had a significant impact on the outcomes of response, relapse or development of cytogenetic abnormalities beyond what is expected with h-ATG/CsA alone1
  • Alemtuzumab has been proposed as an effective salvage strategy for relapsed or refractory severe aplastic anaemia, with response rates of 35–58%2–4
    • A pilot study by the European Group for Blood and Marrow Transplantation (EMBT) found that post-treatment relapse occurred in 85% of patients3
    • However, retreatment with alemtuzumab produced a partial or complete response in 71% of cases3
  • Despite the lack of new non-transplant treatment options, survival has generally improved over the years, particularly among non-responders5
    • Better support during periods of severe neutropenia, particularly the use of empirical antifungal therapy and improved adverse event profiles of newer agents
    • Increasingly successful salvage treatment with repeat IST or HSCT
